About the Game

Five Nights at Derpos (FNaD) is a fan-made horror game inspired by the Five Nights at Freddy’s series. Built entirely in Scratch, FNaD combines classic survival mechanics with unique characters, a creepy atmosphere, and original lore that subtly ties into the wider FNaF universe (if you squint hard enough).

In FNaD, you take on the role of the unlucky night guard at various Derpo's locations — charmingly rundown entertainment venues with more than just bad pizza and broken arcade machines. Your goal is simple: survive five increasingly intense nights by monitoring cameras, managing resources, and staying out of reach of the animatronic nightmares creeping through the halls.

Each installment in the FNaD series features a (not-so-very-unique) twist on FNaF’s original mechanics and gameplay. Whether it’s slightly off-brand camera systems, questionably effective doors, or animatronics with oddly specific movement patterns, FNaD keeps you guessing (or at least mildly confused.)

About the Programming

FNaD was built entirely using Scratch, a visual programming platform designed for creative projects. Despite its blocky simplicity, Scratch packs enough power to create surprisingly complex games.

The game’s core systems (camera switching, power management, animatronic AI, jumpscares, and night transitions) were all crafted with Scratch’s drag-and-drop logic blocks, layered with way too many when green flag clicked scripts. Debugging was... an adventure.

Here’s a quick breakdown of how my workflow usually goes:

  1. Design the sprites – Things usually start as super basic placeholder images, but I always come back and tweak them in my own weird "style"
  2. Program the interactions – Kind of hard to play a game that’s just images... This is the part I’m best at (and honestly the one I have the most fun doing).
  3. Add sounds – I’m not a sound designer, so I’ll be real... I may or may not borrow a few things from FNaF (sorry, Scott!).
  4. Test and refine gameplay – After way too much work, it’s time to make sure the game is actually fun. And challenging. But fair. Hopefully.
